## **The Seven Error Evaluation Standards**

### **Error Type 1: Factual & Common Sense Accuracy**

**Core Principle**: The response must not contain statements that contradict widely accepted common sense or **well-established, static facts** (e.g., geographical locations, physical constants, universally accepted historical events).

**Timeliness Exemption Principle**:
For **highly time-sensitive and frequently changing information** (e.g., the latest price of a product, a company's current CEO, a world record in sports, a celebrity's age on a specific date), a response **should not be penalized as a factual error simply because its internal knowledge is outdated**.

*   **Evaluation Guidelines**:
    *   **Preferred Behavior**: The model's best response is to state its knowledge cutoff date or express uncertainty.
    *   **Acceptable Behavior**: If the model provides information that was **correct at a point in the past but is now outdated**, this should **not** be marked as an error.
    *   **Exception**: Only when the time-sensitive information provided is egregiously wrong (e.g., naming a national leader from decades ago as the current one), and goes beyond what can be reasonably considered "outdated knowledge," can it be judged as a factual error.

*   **Compliant Examples**:
    *   "The Earth revolves around the Sun." (Complies with static facts)
    *   "Water boils at 100°C at standard atmospheric pressure." (Complies with static facts)

*   **Non-compliant Examples**:
    *   **Static Factual Error**: "The capital of China is Shanghai." (This is a stable geographical fact and should be correct.)
    *   **Common Sense Error**: "Humans can survive without oxygen." (This violates basic biological common sense.)

*   **Example NOT Considered an Error (per Timeliness Exemption Principle)**:
    *   **Scenario**: A user asks in 2024: "Who is the current CEO of NVIDIA?"
    *   **Model's Response**: "The CEO of NVIDIA is Jensen Huang."
    *   **Evaluation**: Let's assume for this scenario that Jensen Huang has just stepped down. However, he was the long-time CEO before the model's knowledge cutoff. Because "current CEO" is time-sensitive information, the model's response, while technically outdated, reflects the correct information within its knowledge base. Therefore, per the **Timeliness Exemption Principle**, this response is **not judged as a factual error**.

*   **Note**:
    *   This standard applies to all text content, including the interpretation of charts.
    *   When interpreting a chart, the model must **accurately reflect the data presented within the chart**. Any discrepancy between the model's interpretation and the chart's data is a factual error, and the Timeliness Exemption Principle does not apply, as the chart itself is the immediate source of truth.

### **Error Type 2: Internal Logical Consistency**
Different parts of the response should not contradict each other. Arguments, reasoning, and conclusions must be logically coherent.

*   **Compliant Example**:
    *   "Option A is more costly but has lower risk; Option B is cheaper but comes with significant uncertainty. Therefore, if the budget is sufficient, Option A is recommended."
*   **Non-compliant Examples**:
    *   "This city has no rain year-round and is a famous rainy region." (Self-contradiction)
    *   "Option A is expensive and offers fewer services, making it the best choice." (Conclusion lacks reasonable support)

### **Error Type 3: Chart - Numerical Plausibility**
Data values in the chart or its description must be consistent with real-world logic. It is not required to verify the authenticity of the data source, only to judge if the values are obviously illogical.

*   **Prohibited Items**:
    *   **Unrealistic Values**: An app having the exact same number of users (e.g., 1,234,567) in 2020, 2021, and 2022.
    *   **Proportion Error**: The sum of percentages in a pie chart is strictly not equal to 100%.
    *   **Illogical Negative Values**: A bar chart showing negative "user count" or "sales revenue" (unless explicitly explained as a loss or return).
*   **Non-compliant Examples**:
    *   Pie chart labels: A=40%, B=30%, C=20%, with a total of only 90%.
    *   A sales trend chart shows sales revenue as "-$5 million" (without explaining it's a net adjustment).

### **Error Type 4: Chart - Unit Consistency**
Data with different dimensions or units should not be directly mixed and compared in the same chart, unless there is a clear distinction and explanation (e.g., using a dual Y-axis).

*   **Non-compliant Examples**:
    *   Comparing "phone battery capacity (mAh)" and "phone price (USD)" on the same bar chart.
    *   Plotting "user growth rate (%)" and "total user count (people)" on a single Y-axis line chart.
    *   Using non-numeric labels like "Processor Model: A13, Snapdragon 8 Gen 2" as Y-axis values for plotting.
*   **Compliant Example**:
    *   Using a dual Y-axis chart, clearly labeling the left and right axes with their units, and explaining the purpose of the comparison.

### **Error Type 5: Chart - Information Completeness (Optimized Version)**

**Core Principle**: A chart must contain the essential elements required for a reader to interpret its meaning **without significant ambiguity**. A missing key element that makes the chart difficult or impossible to understand is a violation.

**Contextual Inference Exception**:
An element (such as an axis unit or even a label) is **not considered a critical omission** if its meaning can be **easily and unambiguously inferred** from other chart elements (like the title, data labels) or the surrounding context. The primary test is whether a reasonable person would be confused by the missing information.

*   **Key Elements for Evaluation**:
    *   **Chart Title**: Is it present and descriptive?
    *   **Axis Labels & Units**: Are the axes described? Are the units clear, either explicitly stated or easily inferred?
    *   **Legend**: If multiple data series are present, is there a legend to distinguish them?
    *   **Values**: Are data points or scales present and legible?

*   **Compliant Examples**:
    *   A chart titled **"Annual Sales Revenue (in Millions USD)"** where the Y-axis is labeled "Revenue" but the unit "(in Millions USD)" is omitted *from the axis itself*. The unit is unambiguously provided by the title.
    *   A line chart with an X-axis showing values like "2020, 2021, 2022, 2023". The axis label "Year" is missing, but the format of the values makes the meaning self-evident.

*   **Non-compliant Examples**:
    *   **Ambiguous Omission (Violation)**: A bar chart titled "Quarterly Performance" with an X-axis of "Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4" and a Y-axis showing values "10, 20, 30". Without a unit or a more descriptive label (e.g., "Profit in Thousands," "New Customers"), the chart is uninterpretable. Is it 10 million dollars or 10 new hires?
    *   **Critical Missing Element (Violation)**: A pie chart with multiple colored slices but no legend or labels to explain what each color represents.
    *   **No Chart Provided (Violation)**: The response text says, "As shown in the chart below," but fails to generate any chart.

    *   Using a scatter plot to show the market share of different brands.

### **Error Type 7: Temporal Plausibility (Optimized Version)**

**Core Principle**: Time-related statements must be logically consistent relative to each other and to a defined point in time (the "Reference Date"). Language used (e.g., past tense vs. predictive) should accurately reflect the status of an event relative to this date.

**Defining the 'Reference Date'**: For the purpose of this evaluation, we will use a fixed **Reference Date: August 22, 2025**. This date serves as the "present moment" to distinguish between past and future events.

#### **1. Events Before the Reference Date (Time ≤ Aug 22, 2025)**
*   **Rule**: Must be stated as established facts in the past tense.
*   **Non-compliant Example**: "It is predicted that the company achieved profitability in 2023." (2023 is in the past; its status is a fact, not a prediction.)

#### **2. Events After the Reference Date (Time > Aug 22, 2025)**
*   **Rule**: Must be explicitly framed as non-factual using terms like "forecast," "plan," "estimate," "projected," etc.
*   **Non-compliant Example**: "The new factory opened in October 2025." (October 2025 is in the future relative to the reference date; using a simple past tense implies it has already happened.)

#### **3. Exceptions and Nuances (Important for Plausibility)**

*   **Historical Predictions (Compliant)**:
    *   **Rule**: It is entirely permissible to state a prediction that was made *in the past*, even if the target of that prediction is also in the past. The key is that the language correctly attributes the prediction to its historical context.
    *   **Compliant Example**: "A 2020 analyst report **predicted** that the company **would reach** $1B in revenue by 2024." (This is a valid historical statement about a past prediction.)

*   **General Statements about Spanning Periods (Compliant)**:
    *   **Rule**: General statements about a time period that *includes* the Reference Date (e.g., the full year 2025) are not considered errors if they are plausible and do not create a direct temporal conflict. Such statements often refer to plans, budgets, or strategies for the entire period.
    *   **Compliant Example**: "The company's strategy **for 2025** includes expanding into new markets." (This statement is about the plan for the whole year 2025. It is not an error, even though part of 2025 has already passed the Reference Date.)

#### **4. Period Comparison Consistency**
*   **Rule**: Year-over-year (YoY), quarter-over-quarter (QoQ), etc., comparisons must be based on time periods of the same type and length.
*   **Non-compliant Example**: "Q1 2025 saw a 30% year-over-year increase compared to Q3 2024." (YoY comparison for a quarter must be against the same quarter in the previous year, i.e., Q1 2024.)
